Diagnostic Imaging AI · Citiustech Inc. · Sep 2020 — Oct 2022

Radiology AI Co-Pilot

Directed the large-scale implementation of a deep learning radiology co-pilot that enhanced DICOM image annotation accuracy — improving radiologist efficiency, diagnostic consistency, and throughput across hospital imaging departments.

The Problem

Radiology departments were operating under growing volume pressure — more imaging studies, tighter turnaround expectations, and persistent risk of annotation inconsistency. Manual DICOM review was the primary bottleneck: time-intensive, subject to reader variability, and difficult to scale without adding headcount. The clinical leadership needed an AI system that could augment radiologist workflows without disrupting established reading patterns or clinical trust.

The added complexity was the integration challenge — DICOM systems are not lightweight, and the AI co-pilot had to embed cleanly into the existing PACS (Picture Archiving and Communication System) workflow, not bolt on as a separate tool that radiologists would ignore.

What I Delivered

  • 01
    Clinical Discovery & Workflow Mapping
    • Conducted structured discovery sessions with radiologists, department heads, and IT to map the end-to-end imaging workflow
    • Identified annotation tasks where deep learning would add the highest signal-to-noise — prioritising lesion detection, organ segmentation, and report pre-population
  • 02
    Deep Learning Model Integration
    • Directed integration of a deep learning model stack into the radiology PACS environment
    • Translated clinical accuracy requirements into model evaluation criteria — sensitivity, specificity, false negative thresholds
    • Worked with engineering to define the inference pipeline and annotation overlay UX within the DICOM viewer
  • 03
    DICOM Standards & Interoperability
    • Ensured the AI output layer was fully DICOM-compliant, storing annotation results as DICOM-SR objects
    • Enabled downstream exchange with RIS and EHR systems without format conversion overhead
    • Authored the data exchange specification and validated against live imaging datasets
  • 04
    Confidence Scoring & Override Framework
    • Specified a clinical-grade confidence scoring system surfacing AI certainty levels alongside each annotation
    • Radiologists retained full override capability — the co-pilot was designed as augmentation, not replacement
    • This framing was critical for clinical adoption and regulatory defensibility
  • 05
    Agile Delivery & Stakeholder Management
    • Managed cross-functional delivery across engineering, clinical informatics, compliance, and radiology operations
    • Maintained JIRA boards, sprint planning, and executive KPI reporting throughout
    • Coordinated UAT with radiologist panels and ensured the go-live plan included parallel-read validation
  • 06
    Pre-Authorization Platform Synergy
    • Aligned the radiology AI co-pilot with a parallel computer vision pre-authorization platform
    • Reduced manual document review volume by ~32% through shared infrastructure and unified AI imaging strategy

Retrospective

✓ What Went Well

  • Confidence scoring was the key to radiologist trust — surfacing uncertainty rather than hiding it built credibility fast
  • DICOM-SR output format meant downstream integration required no additional engineering effort
  • Parallel-read validation during UAT caught two annotation edge-cases that would have been missed in a lab-only test
  • Agile sprint structure allowed clinical feedback to reshape annotation priorities mid-development
  • Shared infrastructure with the pre-auth platform cut delivery time and total programme cost

⚠ Challenges

  • PACS vendor APIs were underdocumented — real integration timelines ran 2–3× longer than initially estimated
  • Model retraining cycles on clinical data required more radiologist annotation time than anticipated at scoping
  • Change management for senior radiologists was more intensive than for juniors — required different enablement approaches
  • Compliance review ran sequentially, adding delay that could have been parallelised with engineering sprints

→ Key Lessons

  • Discovery must include a PACS technical deep-dive in week one — vendor API quality is the most common delivery risk in imaging AI
  • Build radiologist annotation time into the project plan as a resource with a budget, not an assumption
  • Frame the co-pilot as workload reduction, not accuracy improvement — clinicians respond to capacity arguments, not technical metrics
  • Run compliance review in parallel with development from sprint one — never as a final gate before go-live
  • Design a parallel-read validation protocol before go-live; it's the most credible way to demonstrate clinical readiness

Applying These Insights to a Future Project

How a Modern PM Would Approach This Differently
With today's AI tooling, here's what I'd do structurally differently — not cosmetically, but from discovery through monitoring.
01

AI-Assisted Clinical Discovery

Use Dovetail AI or GPT-4o to synthesise radiologist interview transcripts and workflow observations in hours. Compress a multi-week discovery phase into days, reserving human judgment for clinical interpretation — not transcript coding.

02

Foundation Model Backbone

Instead of training a purpose-built deep learning model from scratch, I'd fine-tune a medical imaging foundation model (e.g. Google's Med-PaLM or BioViL-T) on the PACS dataset — dramatically reducing annotation data requirements and model iteration cycles.

03

Always-On Anomaly Detection

Deploy Amplitude AI or Datadog from day one to detect co-pilot accuracy degradation, radiologist override rate spikes, and workflow adoption drop-off — replacing reactive weekly KPI reviews with real-time alerting.

04

LLM-Powered Report Pre-Population

Layer a structured LLM (fine-tuned on radiology report language) on top of the annotation output to auto-draft the structured findings section — turning the co-pilot from an annotation tool into a reporting assistant, saving radiologists 5–8 minutes per study.

05

Automated PACS Integration Testing

Use AI-powered test automation (e.g. Testim or Mabl) to generate and run DICOM integration test cases across vendor environments — replacing manual QA cycles and cutting the 2–3× timeline overrun that vendor API gaps caused on this project.

06

Continuous Model Calibration

Build an MLOps pipeline (Azure ML, Vertex AI) from day one that monitors model confidence distribution against radiologist override patterns and triggers targeted retraining — eliminating the ad-hoc retraining cycles that weren't initially budgeted.

The Problem

Insurance claim denials represent one of the highest-cost inefficiencies in hospital revenue cycle management. At Emirates Healthcare Services, denials were being identified only after submission — creating a reactive rework loop that consumed clinical coding staff time, delayed revenue recognition, and obscured patterns that, if detected earlier, could have prevented the denial in the first place.

The challenge was not simply building a predictive model. It required integrating that model into live clinical workflows — parsing CCDA documents in real time, ingesting HL7 and FHIR feeds from multiple EHR systems, and surfacing actionable predictions to coders before a claim left the building. The product had to earn trust across clinical, RCM, and compliance teams simultaneously.

What I Delivered

  • 01
    Scoping & Clinical Requirements
    • Ran structured discovery across clinical operations, RCM leadership, IT, and compliance
    • Translated denial pattern analysis — by payer, procedure type, and coding category — into model feature requirements and data pipeline specifications
    • Produced a product brief, prioritised backlog, and a phased delivery roadmap
  • 02
    HL7 & FHIR EHR Integration
    • Directed HL7 and FHIR integration with EHS's hospital EHR systems for real-time patient and encounter data feeds
    • Authored the data exchange specification and oversaw validation testing across three hospital network sites
  • 03
    CCDA Parsing & Automation Workflows
    • Translated clinical and RCM requirements into automated CCDA document parsing workflows
    • Designed the validation spec — covering edge cases in document structure, missing fields, and coding inconsistencies
    • Enabled the engine to process claims data with sufficient reliability for clinical team action
  • 04
    Predictive Model Design
    • Oversaw design and iteration of the denial prediction model — covering payer-specific rules, procedure-code risk scoring, prior denial history, and documentation completeness signals
    • Defined model evaluation criteria, acceptable precision-recall thresholds, and monitored live performance post-deployment
  • 05
    Executive Dashboards & AI Performance Monitoring
    • Developed real-time executive dashboards covering denial prediction accuracy, claim submission outcomes, revenue recovery trends, and patient volume forecasting
    • Dashboards were adopted as standard RCM reporting instruments across three EHS sites
  • 06
    AI Governance Framework
    • Established an AI governance framework covering model reliability monitoring, audit trails, HIPAA compliance controls, and bias review cadences
    • Defined the protocol for model retraining triggers and version management

Retrospective

✓ What Went Well

  • HL7/FHIR integration framework built here was reused across two subsequent EHS AI projects — significant ROI on the architecture investment
  • CCDA parsing automation achieved sufficient reliability for clinical teams to act on predictions within the pilot window
  • Executive dashboards were adopted as permanent RCM reporting instruments well beyond the original project scope
  • Three enterprise-scale AI transformations delivered on time and within scope against client service orders
  • Governance framework pre-empted compliance concerns and accelerated sign-off across all three sites

⚠ Challenges

  • Payer rule variations across UAE insurers required more custom logic than the initial model architecture anticipated
  • EHR integration timelines consistently ran 2× longer than estimated — vendor cooperation was the primary constraint
  • Clinical coding staff required more active change management than assumed; technical delivery and adoption are different problems
  • Model retraining cycles were not initially budgeted — added as a programme track mid-delivery

→ Key Lessons

  • Start EHR integration discovery in week one — before requirements are finalised. Vendor timelines are the programme's critical path
  • Payer-specific rule libraries must be treated as living documents; build a maintenance process, not just an initial model
  • Run compliance and governance review in parallel with development — sequential review adds weeks with no corresponding quality benefit
  • Budget model retraining as a line item from the start; it's not a phase-two activity, it's ongoing product operation
  • Design the clinical adoption programme before go-live, not after — technical delivery and behavioural change are different projects that need to run in parallel

Applying These Insights to a Future Project

How a Modern PM Would Approach This Differently
With today's AI tooling, here's what I'd do structurally differently — from model design through payer rule management and clinical adoption.
01

LLM-Native Payer Rule Engine

Instead of hard-coding payer rules as static logic, I'd build a LLM-powered rules engine (Azure OpenAI + RAG) that ingests payer policy PDFs and extracts coverage criteria dynamically — eliminating the manual rule maintenance cycle that became a blocker post-launch.

02

AutoML for Faster Model Iteration

Use Vertex AI AutoML or Azure AutoML to generate and compare denial prediction model variants with far less historical data — removing the 18-month data dependency bottleneck that delayed initial model training.

03

FHIR-Native Integration from Day One

Mandate FHIR R4 as the only integration standard and engage EHR vendors in week one with a pre-built FHIR conformance test suite — cutting the 2× timeline overrun that vendor API gaps caused and front-loading the integration risk.

AI-Assisted Clinical Coder Coaching

Layer a real-time LLM coding assistant (similar to Nuance DAX) that explains denial risk signals to coders at the point of claim entry — replacing passive dashboard reporting with contextual, in-workflow guidance that drives behaviour change without change management overhead.

05

Continuous A/B Testing on Intervention Logic

Set up LaunchDarkly or Statsig from day one to run persistent A/B tests on which denial risk thresholds, alert formats, and intervention nudges drive the highest coder action rate — replacing the single pilot-then-scale model with a continuous learning loop.

06

MLOps Pipeline for Retraining

Build an MLOps pipeline (Azure ML Pipelines) that monitors prediction drift against denial outcomes and triggers targeted model retraining when payer rule changes are detected — making retraining a product operation, not a one-off engineering sprint.

The Problem

UK healthcare providers operating under statutory compliance frameworks — including CQC standards, NHS safety obligations, and sector-specific regulatory requirements — face a persistent challenge: compliance data exists, but it is fragmented across systems, difficult to interrogate in real time, and even harder to translate into clear accountability for frontline leaders and executives.

Access Care and Access Compliance, two UK healthcare technology platforms, needed a compliance intelligence layer that would give their client organisations a single, clear view of where they stood against statutory obligations — with automated tracking of audit deadlines, action plan progress, and policy review cycles. The dashboards needed to serve both operational managers (daily compliance activity) and senior leaders (strategic risk oversight), without requiring data literacy to interpret.

What I Delivered

  • 01
    Compliance Framework Mapping
    Conducted structured analysis of UK statutory obligations relevant to each platform's client base — mapping CQC fundamental standards, NHS safety frameworks, and sector-specific requirements into a structured data model. This became the backbone of the dashboard KPI architecture.
  • 02
    Real-Time Compliance Dashboard Design
    Designed and built real-time dashboards covering compliance status by domain, site, and statutory obligation. Dashboard layers were separated by user role: operational managers accessed a daily action view; senior leaders accessed an aggregate risk and trend view. Colour-coded RAG (Red/Amber/Green) status indicators were calibrated to UK inspection criteria.
  • 03
    Automated Audit Deadline Tracking
    Built automated tracking systems to monitor audit deadlines, action plan completion, and policy review cycles — eliminating reliance on manual spreadsheet management. The system generated proactive alerts for approaching deadlines and escalated overdue items through configurable notification workflows.
  • 04
    KPI Framework Aligned to Statutory Obligations
    Defined a KPI framework that mapped directly to UK regulatory reporting requirements, enabling organisations to demonstrate statutory compliance not just internally but to CQC inspectors and commissioners. KPIs were co-designed with healthcare leaders to ensure they reflected how compliance was actually evidenced in practice.
  • 05
    1:1 Leadership Coaching & Capability Building
    Delivered targeted 1:1 coaching to healthcare leaders to strengthen their ability to interpret compliance data, manage accountability structures, and use the dashboards as operational management tools — not just reporting instruments. This was critical to sustaining adoption beyond the implementation phase.
  • 06
    Data Governance & Compliance Frameworks
    Led implementation of data governance projects establishing data ownership, quality standards, and lineage documentation for compliance-critical datasets. Built the technical and process foundation that ensures dashboard data can be trusted for regulatory purposes — not just internal reporting.

Retrospective

✓ What Went Well

  • Role-separated dashboard layers solved the "too much / too little data" problem — managers and leaders got different views of the same underlying data
  • RAG status tied to CQC criteria gave the dashboards immediate credibility with compliance officers — it spoke their language
  • Automated deadline tracking removed the single biggest source of compliance slippage: overlooked review cycles in spreadsheets
  • 1:1 coaching drove adoption far faster than group training sessions had in previous platform rollouts
  • Data governance work front-loaded built trust in dashboard accuracy, which is the primary barrier to leadership adoption

⚠ Challenges

  • UK statutory frameworks are not static — CQC updates require ongoing dashboard and KPI maintenance that needs to be built into the operating model
  • Data quality issues in source systems required governance remediation before dashboards could reflect accurate compliance state
  • Some healthcare leaders were initially resistant to dashboard accountability visibility — required careful stakeholder management
  • Notification fatigue from automated alerts required careful threshold calibration after initial go-live

→ Key Lessons

  • Compliance dashboards are only as credible as the underlying data governance — invest in governance before building the visual layer
  • Design for the inspectors' framework, not just internal reporting preferences — this is what gives dashboards regulatory weight
  • Build a regulatory change management process into the product operating model from day one; UK statutory frameworks evolve
  • Calibrate notification thresholds conservatively at go-live and tune upward — alert fatigue is harder to recover from than under-alerting
  • Leadership coaching is product work, not support — it belongs in the delivery plan and timeline, not as an afterthought

The Problem

A 22% appointment no-show rate across the organisation's network of hospitals, dental centres, public health centres, primary care facilities, and physiotherapy units was eroding revenue, misallocating staff capacity, and degrading service quality for patients who needed earlier access to care. The cause was not well understood — leadership had a symptom, not a diagnosis, and any solution needed to work across a highly heterogeneous facility mix.

A 12-month data audit combined with stakeholder interviews and a 340-respondent patient survey established the baseline and surfaced the root cause mix: forgetting accounted for 38% of no-shows, with the remainder split across transportation barriers, scheduling conflicts, and appointment-related anxiety — each requiring a fundamentally different intervention.

What I Delivered

  • 01
    Discovery & Root Cause Analysis
    • Ran stakeholder interviews, a 12-month historical data audit, and a 340-respondent patient survey to establish the 22% baseline no-show rate
    • Segmented root causes into forgetting, transportation, scheduling conflict, and anxiety-driven avoidance — each mapped to a distinct intervention type
  • 02
    Solution Design & Pilot Preparation
    • Designed five targeted interventions: multi-channel SMS/WhatsApp reminders, self-service one-click rescheduling, an ML-based no-show risk score, an automated waitlist backfill system, and policy/incentive adjustments
    • Built the pilot protocol with a proper control group rather than a single uncontrolled rollout
  • 03
    A/B Pilot at Two Facilities
    • Ran a controlled pilot achieving a 13.4% no-show rate in the treatment group versus 22.1% in control — a 39% relative reduction
    • Same-day cancellations rose 77% in the treatment group, a positive signal that friction was being removed rather than avoided, since each cancellation became a recoverable backfill slot
  • 04
    Phased Rollout Across Facilities
    • Rolled out in waves across 17 hospitals, 8 specialised dental centres, 10 public health centres, 60 primary health care centres, and 2 physiotherapy units
    • Paired each wave with staff training and API integration into the existing scheduling system, plus a live monitoring dashboard tracking no-show rate, fill rate, and reschedule rate
  • 05
    Predictive Risk Scoring & Backfill Automation
    • Built a 50+ variable predictive model to score appointment-level no-show risk, concentrating proactive outreach where it had the highest expected return
    • Connected risk scoring to the waitlist backfill engine so high-risk slots were pre-emptively offered to waitlisted patients ahead of the appointment date
  • 06
    Steady-State Monitoring
    • Stabilised the no-show rate at 11% post-rollout, with $480K in annual revenue recovered and an 88% reduction in manual staff reminder work
    • Patient satisfaction (CSAT) rose from 3.8 to 4.3 out of 5 as rescheduling friction dropped and appointment access improved

Retrospective

✓ What Went Well

  • Dual-touch reminders (48h + 24h) outperformed single reminders by 23% — timing mattered more than channel
  • One-click rescheduling converted no-shows into actionable cancellations, enabling real-time backfill
  • ML risk scoring concentrated proactive outreach where ROI was highest
  • Phased rollout gave each wave time to stabilise before the next began
  • Tracking slot utilisation alongside no-show rate gave a fuller picture of business impact than either metric alone

⚠ Challenges

  • Change management was underestimated — staff buy-in lagged technical delivery by weeks
  • Transportation and anxiety-driven no-shows (~25% combined) weren't meaningfully addressed by reminders alone
  • Loyalty incentives had low uptake — patients didn't perceive them as sufficiently meaningful
  • The ML model required 18 months of historical data, limiting generalisability to newer service lines
  • Voice call outreach had high cost and low incremental lift over SMS alone

→ Key Lessons

  • Behavioural drivers (anxiety, transportation) require fundamentally different interventions than informational ones (forgetting) — segment before designing solutions
  • Friction removal matters more than message content — making rescheduling effortless beat crafting the perfect reminder copy
  • Staff are stakeholders, not just users — involve and train them in parallel with technical development, not after go-live
  • Design the full cancellation-to-backfill loop from day one, not as a phase-two add-on; every cancellation is a recoverable slot
  • Executive dashboards and operational PM dashboards should be deliberately different artefacts — leadership needs business impact, PMs need diagnostic detail

The Problem

Pre-authorization and claims workflows were fragmented across EHRs, payer portals, and manual document queues, with no single operational layer connecting intake to payer decision. Authorization specialists and claims reviewers spent the bulk of their time on manual document review rather than judgement-intensive case work, while denial patterns and SLA bottlenecks across high-volume specialties went largely undiagnosed until they showed up as lost revenue.

The platform needed to unify document extraction, eligibility validation, payer-rule logic, and denial risk scoring into a single intelligent layer — without asking clinical and revenue cycle staff to trust a black box, in a HIPAA-regulated environment where every AI-assisted decision required an audit trail.

Retrospective

✓ What Went Well

  • A configurable rules engine absorbed frequent payer rule changes without requiring code deploys — the most operationally durable architectural decision
  • Human-in-the-loop review preserved clinical trust during early model adoption and accelerated sign-off
  • Phased pilot by specialty reduced scope complexity and enabled genuinely useful UAT feedback before enterprise rollout
  • Denial risk scoring gave revenue cycle teams early warning before submission, catching high-risk cases that previously only surfaced post-denial
  • SLA + risk-based work queue prioritisation cut manual prioritisation effort and gave supervisors a real-time operational view

⚠ Challenges

  • EHR and payer integration timelines were consistently underestimated — interface discovery needed to start in week one, not after requirements were finalised
  • NLP model accuracy on handwritten and low-quality scanned documents was lower than expected, requiring a manual fallback queue that absorbed more capacity than planned
  • Compliance sign-off ran sequentially rather than in parallel with development, creating a three-week bottleneck before the pilot could launch
  • Payer portal variability required significantly more per-payer customisation than the initial scope assumed
  • Clinical staff trust in AI recommendations built slowly — adoption lagged technical readiness by several weeks despite training completion

→ Key Lessons

  • Compliance review must run in parallel with development sprints — treating it as a final gate adds weeks and compresses the pilot window unnecessarily
  • Model explainability is a clinical trust requirement, not a nice-to-have — confidence scores and override logs belong in the MVP, not a later release
  • Build the payer rules engine before the AI extraction layer — deterministic logic is the foundation on which AI recommendations earn clinical credibility
  • Data quality assessment must precede model training — build a formal data-quality gate into discovery, not after scoping is complete
  • Integration discovery with EHRs and payer systems consistently takes 2× longer than estimated — start immediately, regardless of requirements readiness

Applying These Insights to a Future Project

How a Modern PM Would Approach This Differently
Foundation model capabilities and clinical LLM maturity have fundamentally shifted what's possible — structurally, not cosmetically.
01

Fine-Tuned Foundation Models for Extraction

Replace custom OCR + NLP pipelines with fine-tuned vision-language models on clinical document types — dramatically improving accuracy on the handwritten and variable-format inputs that broke the original pipeline.

02

AI-Assisted HL7/FHIR Mapping

Use AI-assisted integration tooling to accelerate discovery and field mapping — compressing weeks of manual HL7 mapping into days and reducing the per-payer customisation overhead that extended the original timeline.

03

Real-Time Payer Policy Monitoring

Deploy an AI agent to continuously monitor payer policy portals and clinical guideline databases for rule changes — replacing the reactive review process that caused mid-flight rules engine rework.

04

LLM-Generated Appeal Packets

Use a clinically fine-tuned LLM to auto-draft appeal letters and prior authorization justification packets from structured case data — a capability that was a wave-2 roadmap item but could now be part of the MVP.

05

AI Copilot for Authorization Specialists

Embed an in-workflow AI assistant that surfaces relevant payer rules, missing-document checklists, and denial risk rationale in natural language — replacing static training decks with contextual, real-time guidance.

06

Automated Governance & Drift Detection

Use model monitoring tooling to flag data drift and demographic bias automatically — replacing manual quarterly model reviews with always-on governance that satisfies compliance requirements continuously.
